01 - Overview

What is an AML scan?

An AML scan is a crypto AML review of a wallet address or transaction hash for possible exposure to sanctions, illicit services, fraud, stolen funds, or other high-risk activity.

In crypto, AML scanning usually means using blockchain analytics to trace public on-chain activity and classify risk signals around an address. It is also called wallet risk screening, address screening, or blockchain address risk review.

The output is not a legal finding and does not prove that a wallet owner committed wrongdoing. It gives compliance teams, traders, OTC desks, and individual users a structured way to review on-chain context before deciding whether to continue, pause, or investigate further.

02 - How it works

How an AML scan works

A scan AML workflow takes a wallet address or transaction hash, traces on-chain exposure, checks risk datasets and sanctions lists, then presents signals for human review.

Blockchain analytics tools use transaction graphs, attribution datasets, clustering heuristics, typologies, and sanctions-list references to identify possible links between an address and known risk categories.

Chainsift keeps the workflow practical: enter the subject, review the wallet risk indicators, and decide whether to proceed, request more information, document the review, or escalate to a qualified compliance professional.

03 - Signals

What an AML scan checks for

An AML scan checks for risk signals such as sanctioned addresses, mixers, darknet-market exposure, scams, stolen funds, and high-risk exchange activity.

Risk indicators are usually based on proximity, direct or indirect exposure, timing, value flow, clustering, and whether a wallet has interacted with services or entities that appear in recognized risk datasets.

Signals should be read as indicators, not verdicts. A high-risk signal can reflect direct exposure, indirect exposure, stale attribution, or legitimate activity that still requires a closer look.

Sanctioned addresses

Potential links to addresses, entities, or services associated with sanctions-list data, including OFAC SDN-related screening where applicable.

Mixers/tumblers

Exposure to services designed to obscure transaction trails, which can increase compliance review needs even when activity is not conclusive by itself.

Darknet markets

Direct or indirect interaction with wallets attributed to darknet-market activity in blockchain analytics datasets.

Scams/fraud

Signals tied to reported fraud, phishing, impersonation, investment scams, or other abuse patterns that may require enhanced review.

Stolen funds

Exposure to wallets or flows associated with hacks, exploits, theft events, or laundering paths identified by risk datasets.

High-risk exchange exposure

Interaction with services that may present elevated risk because of weak controls, risky counterparties, or other compliance concerns.

04 - Standards

AML standards & regulations for crypto AML screening

Crypto AML screening is shaped by standards and rules from bodies such as FATF, OFAC, FinCEN, and EU AML frameworks, but obligations depend on jurisdiction and business model.

The FATF Travel Rule focuses on originator and beneficiary information for virtual asset transfers involving virtual asset service providers. OFAC sanctions screening is relevant when exposure may involve the SDN List or other sanctions programs.

FinCEN guidance is important for U.S. money services business analysis involving convertible virtual currency. In the European Union, AML directives and the broader AML/CFT framework shape how firms approach customer due diligence, suspicious activity reporting, and risk-based controls.

Chainsift is not a regulator, sanctions authority, law firm, or substitute for a compliance program. Use an AML scan as one input in a documented risk-based process.

05 - Landscape

AML scanning tools & data providers

The AML scanning landscape includes blockchain analytics and crypto compliance providers that help teams automate address screening, monitor exposure, and investigate escalations.

Market context includes providers such as Chainalysis, TRM Labs, Elliptic, and Crystal Intelligence. These companies are commonly discussed in crypto compliance because they provide blockchain intelligence, wallet screening, transaction monitoring, or investigative tooling.

Manual review can work for occasional checks, but it is slow and easy to apply inconsistently. Automated AML scan workflows help standardize first-pass screening, keep evidence organized, and route higher-risk cases to deeper review.

06 - Limits

Limitations & responsible use of an AML scan

An AML scan is useful for risk triage, but it can produce false positives and false negatives, and blockchain heuristics are not proof of ownership, intent, or criminal activity.

Address clustering, attribution, and typology labels can be incomplete or change as new information becomes available. Indirect exposure can also be hard to interpret without context such as business purpose, counterparty information, timing, and the user's risk appetite.

Privacy matters. Only submit information you are permitted to review, store, or process, and avoid treating a public wallet lookup as a complete identity check. Compliance decisions, transaction decisions, and legal obligations remain with the user.

AML risk signals are heuristic, not proof. Expect false positives, confirm context, and treat a scan as one input — not a final compliance decision.
FAQ

AML scan FAQ

What is an AML scan?

An AML scan is a review of a crypto wallet address or transaction hash for possible anti-money-laundering risk signals. It uses blockchain analytics and address screening to check whether the wallet has direct or indirect exposure to sanctions, scams, darknet markets, mixers, stolen funds, or other high-risk categories. The result is a risk indicator, not proof of identity, intent, or unlawful activity.

How do I scan a wallet for AML risk?

To scan AML risk, paste the wallet address or transaction hash into the screening tool, confirm the chain if required, and review the returned risk signals. Look at the type of exposure, whether it is direct or indirect, and whether the finding is recent or historical. For business use, document the review and route higher-risk results through your internal compliance process.

Is an AML address scan accurate?

An AML address scan can be useful, but it is heuristic and not proof. Blockchain analytics depend on attribution, clustering, sanctions data, typology labels, and transaction graph analysis, all of which can be incomplete or change over time. A scan may miss activity, misclassify exposure, or flag a wallet that has a legitimate explanation. Treat the output as decision support.

What does a high-risk result mean?

A high-risk result means the wallet or transaction has signals that merit closer review. It may indicate direct exposure to a risky entity, indirect exposure through previous hops, interaction with a mixer, or a match to a sanctions or illicit-activity dataset. It does not automatically mean the current holder is criminal. Review the signal type, context, and your obligations before acting.

Is scanning an address legal/private?

Public blockchain addresses can often be reviewed on-chain, but privacy and legal considerations still matter. Do not submit customer data, counterparty details, or internal notes unless you have a lawful basis and appropriate controls. Individuals may use address screening for personal risk awareness; businesses should align scans with privacy notices, data retention rules, and compliance policies.

Do I need an AML scan as an individual vs business?

Individuals may use an AML scan to check wallet risk before receiving funds, interacting with a counterparty, or reviewing a suspicious transaction. Businesses usually need a more formal process: customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, sanctions screening, escalation procedures, and recordkeeping where applicable. Whether a scan is required depends on your jurisdiction, activity, and role in the transaction.
